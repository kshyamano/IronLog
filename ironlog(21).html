📖 Understanding Your Dashboard
1
Hero Card — Today's Overview
The top card greets you with the time of day and shows your current streak. The START WORKOUT button takes you directly to today's log. The pill below it shows your current training split (Push / Pull / Legs / Rest) if you have a plan active.
2
Stat Cards
Five numbers that summarise your entire training history at a glance:
Active Days — total number of days you've trained
Volume — lifetime total of weight × reps across all exercises (in units/100)
Total Sets — every set ever logged
Best e1RM — your highest estimated 1-rep max across all lifts
Streak — consecutive days trained. Miss a day and it resets to zero.
3
Activity Heatmap — Last 13 Weeks
84 small squares, one per day. Each square's colour shows how much volume you trained that day — darker and richer means more work. The colour also reflects the dominant muscle group trained. Rest days are flat grey. Hover a square to see the exact date, muscles, and volume. Click any trained day to jump to that session's log.
4
This Week — Activity Rings
Six rings showing how balanced your training was this week across muscle groups (Upper / Lower / Push / Pull / Core / Cardio). A full ring means you hit your weekly target for that category. The goal is to have all rings lit by Sunday. Gaps tell you what you haven't trained yet this week.
5
30-Day Volume Trend
A line chart showing your daily training volume for the last 30 days. An upward trend over time means progressive overload — you're doing more work than before. Flat lines mean maintenance. Drops can signal deload weeks, illness, or missed sessions. This is your most honest indicator of whether you're making progress.
6
All-Time PRs — e1RM
Your personal records. Each entry shows your strongest ever estimated 1-rep max for that exercise. These update automatically whenever you log a set that beats the previous best. Aim to beat at least one PR every 2–3 weeks — that's the clearest sign of strength progress.
7
Muscle Map — Interactive Anatomy
An anatomical figure showing both the front and back of your body. Each muscle region lights up in its colour based on how much you've trained it — bright and saturated means lots of volume, dark grey means undertrained or never trained. Hover any muscle to see your lifetime volume, best e1RM, last trained date, and sets this week. Click any muscle to jump to History filtered for that muscle group.
8
Recent Sessions
Your last 5 sessions shown as a compact list. Each row shows the date, exercises trained, total volume, and sets. Tap any row to open that session's full log. Tap VIEW ALL → to go to the full History journal.

📖 How to Log a Workout
1
Tap "ADD EXERCISE"
The big button at the bottom opens the exercise library. Exercises are grouped by muscle — Chest, Back, Shoulders, etc. Tap any exercise to add it to today's log. You can also search by name or add your own custom exercise.
2
Log a Set
Each exercise card has three fields:
WEIGHT KG — how much you lifted (use 0 for bodyweight)
REPS — how many repetitions you completed
RPE — Rate of Perceived Exertion (1–10). How hard did that set feel? 6 = easy, 8 = hard but 2 reps left, 10 = absolute maximum. Leave blank if you don't use RPE.
Then tap + SET to record it.
3
What is e1RM?
Estimated 1-Rep Max — a calculation that estimates the heaviest single rep you could do based on the weight and reps you just lifted. Formula: weight × (1 + reps ÷ 30). If you lift 80 kg × 10 reps, your e1RM ≈ 107 kg. IRONLOG tracks this over time so you can see your strength progress even if you never actually test your max.
4
Use the Set Timer (optional)
Tap ⏱ Use set timer on any exercise to enable it. Then:
▶ START SET — starts counting how long your set takes
■ SET DONE — stops the set clock, starts counting rest time
▶ NEXT SET — records the rest, starts the next set
✓ EXERCISE DONE — starts a purple inter-exercise rest timer
This data is saved with each set and used to calculate your training intensity (Low / Med / High / Peak).
5
Supersets
A superset means doing two exercises back-to-back with no rest in between — like Bench Press then immediately Tricep Pushdown. At the bottom of any exercise card tap ⚡ Pick from exercise library to pair it with another exercise. They'll be grouped and labelled (i) and (ii).
6
Synergist Muscles
Under each exercise name you'll see coloured pills — these show which muscles are also being worked based on EMG research. For example, Bench Press works your Chest (100%) but also your Shoulders (50%) and Triceps (55%). IRONLOG logs this secondary fatigue automatically — it shows up on your muscle map on the dashboard.
7
Save Your Session
Once you've logged at least one set, a gold 🏁 SAVE SESSION button appears. Tap it to give your session a title, write notes (injuries, how it felt, PRs), and rate it 1–5 stars. After saving, your session appears in History and on the Dashboard heatmap and charts.

📖 Understanding Your Weekly View
1
Navigate Between Weeks
Use the ← Prev and Next → buttons to move between weeks. The label shows the date range of the current week (Monday to Sunday). You can go back as far as your training history goes to compare old weeks with new ones.
2
Day Pills — Mon to Sun
Seven pill buttons, one per day of the week. Days where you trained are highlighted with a coloured dot at the bottom. Today's pill has a gold border. Tap any trained day to open its full log. Rest days are plain and dim. Aim to have at least 3–5 active days per week depending on your programme.
3
Weekly Summary Cards
Four numbers that summarise the whole week:
Active Days — how many days you trained out of 7
Total Sets — all sets logged across the week
Volume — total weight × reps for the week (u/100)
Exercises — unique exercises performed this week
Compare these numbers week-over-week to spot if your workload is growing, staying flat, or dropping.
4
Daily Volume Chart
A bar chart showing how much volume (weight × reps) you did each day this week. Taller bars = harder sessions. This helps you see if your training load is spread evenly or heavily front-loaded. Most programmes aim for balanced distribution rather than two huge days and five rest days.
5
Volume by Muscle Group
A bar chart showing how much total volume each muscle group received this week — including synergist fatigue. For example, if you did Bench Press, your Chest bar will be tallest, but Shoulders and Triceps also get a portion automatically based on EMG research. This gives you the most honest picture of muscle balance and whether you're neglecting anything.
6
Using It for Planning
The weekly view is your planning tool. If you see that Back volume is low mid-week, schedule a pull session. If legs are empty all week, add a squat or deadlift day. Compare the current week's volume chart with last week to make sure you're doing slightly more over time — that consistent small increase is what drives muscle growth and strength gain.

📖 Progress Charts
1
Pick an Exercise
Use the EXERCISE dropdown to select any movement you have logged at least once. Only exercises with recorded data appear in this list. If the list is empty, log some sessions first in the LOG tab.
2
Choose a Time Range
Filter by the last 30, 60, 90 days, or all-time. Shorter ranges show recent form and momentum. All-time reveals the long arc of your strength development — this is where you see how far you've come since you first started.
3
e1RM Trend Line
The main chart plots your estimated 1-Rep Max over time. Each point is your best set of the day, converted to a theoretical max using the Epley formula (weight × (1 + reps ÷ 30)). A rising line = getting stronger. A flat line = stalled — time to change something. A dip = fatigue, deload, or form check needed.
4
Volume per Session
The second chart shows total volume (weight × reps across all sets) every time you trained that exercise. Volume is the primary driver of muscle growth. Aim for a gradual upward trend — even adding one set or 2.5 kg per week compounds significantly over months.
5
Reading Plateaus
If your e1RM line has been flat for 3+ weeks, try one of these: add one more set per session, increase weight by 2.5 kg, change rep range (e.g. 5×5 instead of 3×10), add a deload week, or improve sleep and nutrition. Progress stalls are normal — recognising them early is the key to breaking through.

MONTHLY 1RM GOALS
📖 Muscle Strength Targets
1
What Are Muscle Targets?
A target is the estimated 1-rep max (e1RM) you want to reach for each muscle group this month. For example, setting a Chest target of 120 kg means you want your best Bench Press e1RM to hit 120 kg by end of month. IRONLOG tracks your actual best e1RM per muscle group automatically from your logged sessions.
2
Setting a Target
Tap Set target next to any muscle. Enter the e1RM you're aiming for in kg. A progress bar fills as your actual best e1RM approaches the target. Green = at or above target. The bar resets every month so you can set fresh goals.
3
Setting Realistic Targets
A good rule of thumb for beginners: aim for a 2–5% increase in e1RM per month. Intermediate lifters: 1–2% per month. If you're already at the target, raise it. If you're consistently below, either train that muscle more or lower the target — realistic goals beat demoralising ones every time.
4
Which Muscle to Prioritise?
Look at which muscles have the lowest progress bars compared to others — those are your weakest links. Lagging muscles limit overall performance. For example, weak glutes limit squat depth and deadlift power. Prioritise lagging groups by adding one extra session per week and placing them first in your workout when energy is highest.
GLOSSARY
e1RMEstimated 1-rep max calculated from your working sets
TargetYour monthly strength goal for a given muscle group
LaggingA muscle group significantly weaker than your others
PriorityTraining a weak muscle first in a session for max effort

Set monthly estimated 1RM targets per muscle. Click "Set target" to update.

📖 Trainer Feature
1
Are you a Trainer or a Student?
IRONLOG has two roles. Trainers can monitor multiple students' workout history, volume, and progress remotely. Students can connect to a trainer so their sessions are visible to that person. Your role is set when you register — trainers check the "I'm a Gym Trainer" box on sign-up. You cannot change it after without creating a new account.
2
If You're a Student — Adding a Trainer
Enter your trainer's email address and tap Send Request. Your trainer will see a pending request on their dashboard and can accept or decline. Once accepted, they can view your full session history, volume trends, and best lifts. You can remove the connection at any time from this page — it immediately revokes their access.
3
If You're a Trainer — Accepting Students
Pending requests appear as dashed cards at the top. Tap ✓ Accept Student to confirm the connection, or Decline to reject it. Accepted students appear in your student grid. Tap any student card to view their training data — sessions, total sets, best e1RM, and the 5 most recent workouts.
4
What Trainers Can See
For each connected student you can see: total sessions logged, all-time sets, their best e1RM and which exercise it's on, and their 5 most recent workouts with date, muscles, volume, and set count. This gives you enough to assess consistency, volume trends, and whether they're hitting their programme. Use the Open Full Log button inside a session to see every set, weight, and rest time.
5
Privacy
Your workout data is only ever shared with a trainer you explicitly connect to. IRONLOG never shows your data to anyone else. The connection is mutual — both sides must agree. If you remove a trainer connection, they immediately lose access to all your data. No data is deleted from your account when you disconnect — it only affects visibility.

🔬 EXERCISE RESEARCH

📖 Exercise Research
1
What Is EMG Data?
EMG stands for Electromyography — a technique that measures electrical activity in muscles during exercise. Researchers attach sensors to specific muscles and record how hard each one works at different points of a lift. The result is a percentage of Maximum Voluntary Isometric Contraction (%MVIC) — essentially how activated a muscle is compared to its absolute maximum. IRONLOG uses this data to automatically log secondary muscle fatigue.
2
Synergist Fatigue — What It Means for You
When you bench press, your Chest is the primary mover — but your Shoulders (anterior deltoid) activate at ~50% and your Triceps at ~55% of their max. IRONLOG records this automatically. So every Bench Press set adds volume to Chest, Shoulders, and Triceps simultaneously. This means your Muscle Map and volume charts reflect real total fatigue — not just what you directly targeted.
3
Reading the Research Cards
Each paper card shows: the study title, the journal it was published in, the year, a plain-English finding summary, and a DOI link to the original paper. The muscle activation bars show the %MVIC for each muscle at peak contraction. These are the coefficients IRONLOG uses to calculate synergist volume in your training data.
4
Exercise Variants Matter
Incline Bench shifts more stress to the upper chest and anterior deltoid (+40% shoulder activation). Decline Bench maximises the lower/sternal pec and reduces shoulder involvement (−35%). IRONLOG has different EMG coefficients for each variant — so your muscle map correctly reflects whether you favour flat, incline, or decline pressing in your programme.

EMGElectromyography — measures muscle electrical activity
%MVIC% of maximum muscle activation. 100% = that muscle's absolute max
SynergistA muscle that assists the primary mover during a lift
CoefficientThe fraction of volume credited to a secondary muscle

→
METHODOLOGY NOTE
All EMG values are normalised to %MVIC (percentage of maximal voluntary isometric contraction) measured with surface electrodes. This method is standard in exercise EMG research but has limitations: electrode placement, individual anatomy, fatigue state, and load intensity all influence readings. IRONLOG uses population-averaged values from multiple studies as approximations, not precise individual measurements. The synergist fatigue system is intended to improve relative training balance awareness, not to give absolute physiological measurements. If you train with a predominantly narrow grip or significant incline work, the actual triceps and deltoid contributions will be higher than these coefficients suggest. Paper links open Google Scholar searches to locate the original publication.

METHODOLOGY NOTE
Squat EMG data is highly sensitive to stance width, bar position (high vs low bar), heel elevation, and individual anthropometry. Studies use varied normalisation protocols — values here reflect averaged %MVIC from multiple surface-electrode studies at approximately parallel depth with hip-width stance. Wide-stance or sumo-style squatters will see higher glute and adductor activation. FARMER
⚡ HYBRID EXERCISE ANALYSIS
FARMER WALK
Bilateral loaded carry — dumbbells, kettlebells, trap bar, or farmer handles — walked for distance or time at maximal sustainable load. WHY "HYBRID"?
The Farmer Walk is unclassifiable under a single primary muscle because no one muscle dominates. The trapezius, forearm flexors, lumbar erectors, and hip extensors all operate at near-maximal output simultaneously — each competing for the title of limiting factor. IRONLOG logs it as Hybrid and distributes volume to all six involved systems proportionally by %MVIC share.
